Yes — definitely more than 1,000 QSOs — but how much more depends on your target use case.

Given what you’re building with your Scala / ScalaFX Field Day logging ecosystem, here’s a practical sizing guide.

⸻

📊 Realistic QSO Volumes by Station Type

🏠 Small / Casual / Home Stations
	•	Typical: 200–1,500 QSOs
	•	Single operator or small family setup
	•	Low concurrency
	•	Simple logging OK

⛺ Club Portable (2A–4A class)
	•	Typical: 1,500–6,000 QSOs
	•	Multiple transmitters
	•	Multiple logging positions
	•	Concurrent log writes
	•	Network sync required

🏆 Large / High-Performance Stations
	•	Typical: 6,000–10,000+ QSOs
	•	Multi-op contest-grade stations
	•	Parallel operators
	•	High rate bursts (150–250 QSOs/hour per transmitter)

⸻

⚡ Rate Matters More Than Total

Your logger design should handle burst rates, not just totals.

Peak observed rates at big sites:
	•	3 transmitters × 200 QSOs/hour = 600 QSOs/hour
	•	That’s:
	•	10 QSOs/min
	•	One every 6 seconds
	•	With multiple operators entering simultaneously.

For distributed logging (like your FDLog / FdSwarm style system):

You should design for:
	•	✅ Concurrent writes
	•	✅ Conflict-free merge
	•	✅ Network partitions
	•	✅ Replay / journal recovery
	•	✅ Duplicate detection at scale

⸻

🧠 Practical Engineering Target (Recommended)

For a modern Field Day logging app:

Design capacity targets:
	•	📦 15,000 QSOs per event
	•	👥 10–20 concurrent operators
	•	🔁 Real-time replication across nodes
	•	💾 Append-only journal safe to ~50k records
	•	⚡ UI filtering/search under 50 ms at 10k records

That gives you comfortable headroom beyond typical Field Day usage.
